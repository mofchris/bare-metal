## Scientific notation, in hardware

A float is a number written as ± mantissa × 2^exponent, packed into fixed
bits. For **float32**:

| Field    | Bits | Job                                      |
| -------- | ---- | ---------------------------------------- |
| sign     | 1    | plus or minus                            |
| exponent | 8    | the scale: which power of two (bias 127) |
| mantissa | 23   | the digits: precision within that scale  |

Two consequences fall straight out of the layout:

1. **Range comes from the exponent.** 8 bits of exponent span roughly
   10^−38 to 10^+38.
2. **Precision comes from the mantissa.** 23 stored bits (plus one implicit
   leading 1 — normalized numbers always start with 1, so it isn't stored)
   ≈ 24 significant binary digits ≈ **about 7 decimal digits**.

Keep "range vs precision" separate in your head; the next lesson's entire
story (fp16 vs bf16) is about how these two budgets get split when bits are
scarce.

## 0.1 does not exist

In binary, 1/10 is a repeating fraction — exactly like 1/3 in decimal. So
`0.1` stored as a float is a nearby number, not 0.1, and
`0.1 + 0.2 == 0.3` is famously false in every language. This isn't a bug —
it's the representation. The professional habits that follow: never compare
floats with `==`; compare against a tolerance, and choose the tolerance
consciously (that's what the labs' harness does when checking your numeric
results).

## Floats are spaced, and the spacing grows

There are as many floats between 1 and 2 as between 1024 and 2048 — the
mantissa provides a fixed number of steps _per power of two_. So the gap
between adjacent floats (the "unit in the last place") is tiny near zero and
huge near 10^38. **Machine epsilon** — the gap just above 1.0 — is about
1.2×10⁻⁷ for float32: add anything much smaller than that to 1.0 and the
answer is still exactly 1.0. That absorption effect is the seed of half the
failure modes in lesson 04, and you'll trigger it on purpose in lab L3.1.

## Rounding is the default state of affairs

Nearly every operation's true result falls between two representable floats
and gets rounded (default mode: round-to-nearest-even). One rounding is
harmless — ~10⁻⁷ relative error. The interesting questions, which the rest
of this module chases, are: what happens after a billion of them, and what
happens when the bits get scarcer.

## Special values, briefly

The exponent's all-zeros and all-ones patterns are reserved: they encode
**±0, ±infinity** (overflow's destination), **NaN** (not-a-number: 0/0,
∞−∞ — and NaN ≠ NaN, by design), and **subnormals** (a graceful ramp of
tiny values below the normal range, at reduced precision). You'll meet inf
and NaN again in lesson 04 as the visible symptoms of numerical trouble in
training runs.
