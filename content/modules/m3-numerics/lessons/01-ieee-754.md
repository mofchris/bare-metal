## Scientific notation, in hardware

A float is the computer's version of scientific notation: a number written as
± **mantissa** × 2^**exponent**, where the mantissa carries the digits and the
exponent says where the point sits. In decimal you'd write 6.02 × 10²³; a
float does the same in base two, because the hardware only has 0s and 1s.

Both parts get packed into a fixed number of bits, and that fixed budget is
the whole drama of this module: one pot of bits, split between two jobs that
both want more.

For **float32** the 32 bits are divided:

| Field    | Bits | Job                                      |
| -------- | ---- | ---------------------------------------- |
| sign     | 1    | plus or minus                            |
| exponent | 8    | the scale: which power of two (bias 127) |
| mantissa | 23   | the digits: precision within that scale  |

"Bias 127" is how the exponent covers negative powers without needing its own
minus sign: whatever 8-bit value is stored, subtract 127 to get the real
exponent. A stored 128 means 2¹, a stored 126 means 2⁻¹. One unsigned field,
both directions.

Two consequences fall straight out of the layout:

1. **Range comes from the exponent.** 8 bits of exponent span roughly
   10^−38 to 10^+38 — how big and how small a number can get.
2. **Precision comes from the mantissa.** 23 stored bits, plus one implicit
   leading 1 that isn't stored at all: in base two every number can be shifted
   so its leading digit is 1 (there's no other option for a nonzero digit), so
   that 1 is guaranteed and storing it would waste a bit. Numbers in this
   shifted form are called **normalized**. The result is ≈ 24 significant
   binary digits ≈ **about 7 decimal digits** of precision.

Keep "range vs precision" separate in your head; the next lesson's entire
story (fp16 vs bf16) is about how these two budgets get split when bits are
scarce.

## 0.1 does not exist

In binary, 1/10 is a repeating fraction — exactly like 1/3 in decimal, where
0.333… never terminates no matter how many digits you allow. So `0.1` stored
as a float is a nearby number, not 0.1, and `0.1 + 0.2 == 0.3` is famously
false in every language.

This isn't a bug — it's the representation. The professional habits that
follow: never compare floats with `==`; compare against a tolerance (is the
difference smaller than some threshold you chose?), and choose that tolerance
consciously rather than copying one. That's exactly what the labs' harness
does when checking your numeric results.

## Floats are spaced, and the spacing grows

There are as many floats between 1 and 2 as between 1024 and 2048 — the
mantissa provides a fixed number of steps _per power of two_, and doubling the
exponent doubles how much ground those same steps have to cover. So the gap
between adjacent floats is tiny near zero and enormous near 10^38. That gap
has a name, the **unit in the last place** (ULP): the distance to the next
representable float, which depends on where you are on the number line.

**Machine epsilon** is the specific ULP just above 1.0 — about 1.2×10⁻⁷ for
float32. Add anything much smaller than that to 1.0 and the answer is still
exactly 1.0: the true result falls between two representable floats and gets
rounded back to where it started. That absorption effect is the seed of half
the failure modes in lesson 04, and you'll trigger it on purpose in lab L3.1.

## Rounding is the default state of affairs

Nearly every operation's true result falls between two representable floats
and gets rounded. The default rule is **round-to-nearest-even**: go to the
nearer of the two, and when the value sits exactly halfway, pick the one whose
last bit is 0. That tie-break exists so that halfway cases don't all round the
same direction — consistent rounding-up would make a long sum drift steadily
upward.

One rounding is harmless — around 10⁻⁷ relative error, meaning error measured
as a fraction of the value rather than an absolute amount. The interesting
questions, which the rest of this module chases, are: what happens after a
billion of them, and what happens when the bits get scarcer.

## Special values, briefly

The exponent's all-zeros and all-ones bit patterns are reserved rather than
meaning what the formula says. They encode **±0**, **±infinity** (where
overflow lands when a result is too big for the format), **NaN** (not-a-number
— the result of 0/0 or ∞−∞, and famously NaN ≠ NaN, by design, so testing a
value against itself is how you detect one), and **subnormals** (a graceful
ramp of very tiny values below the normal range, bought by giving up the
implicit leading 1 and therefore precision).

You'll meet inf and NaN again in lesson 04 as the visible symptoms of
numerical trouble in training runs.
