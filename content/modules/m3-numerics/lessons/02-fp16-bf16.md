## Why shrink numbers at all?

Lesson 01 gave float32 ~7 digits — far more precision than a neural network
needs (its inputs are noisy, its weights tolerate perturbation by design).
Meanwhile M1 taught that most tensor ops are **memory-bound**. Halving the
bytes per number: halves memory traffic (doubling effective arithmetic
intensity on the roofline — the same op moves right on the plot), halves
memory footprint (bigger models/batches fit), and unlocks hardware that
multiplies narrow numbers faster. Precision is a _budget you spend_; the
question is only where to cut.

## Two ways to split 16 bits

| Format   | Exponent | Mantissa | Range                                  | Precision (~decimal) |
| -------- | -------- | -------- | -------------------------------------- | -------------------- |
| float32  | 8        | 23       | ~10^±38                                | ~7 digits            |
| **fp16** | 5        | 10       | max **65504**, smallest normal ~6×10⁻⁵ | ~3 digits            |
| **bf16** | 8        | 7        | ~10^±38 (same as fp32)                 | ~2–3 digits          |

Same 16 bits, opposite philosophies. **fp16** keeps decent precision but
pays with a tiny range: anything past 65504 overflows to infinity, and small
values slide into underflow. **bf16** is float32 with the last 16 mantissa
bits sawn off: the _same_ exponent field, so the same enormous range —
paying with precision so coarse that 256 + 1 rounds back to 256.

## Why range beat precision in training

Training's problem children are **gradients**: they span wild magnitudes,
shrinking toward zero as models converge and occasionally spiking. In fp16,
small gradients underflow to zero (learning silently stops in those weights)
and spikes overflow to inf (the loss goes NaN). Mixed-precision training
with fp16 works — Micikevicius et al. showed it — but only with **loss
scaling** (lesson 04) constantly shepherding values into fp16's narrow
window.

bf16's fp32-sized range makes the shepherding unnecessary: nothing that fit
in fp32 overflows or underflows in bf16. The coarse mantissa turns out to be
fine because gradient _noise_ already exceeds rounding error, and the
accumulations that do need care are done in fp32 anyway (accumulate wide,
store narrow — the standard pattern). That practical simplicity is why bf16,
introduced for TPUs, became the training default across modern hardware.

## The pattern to remember

Modern precision policy in one sentence: **store and move tensors narrow;
accumulate wide**. Matmul units consume bf16/fp16 inputs but sum products
into fp32 accumulators; optimizer state often stays fp32 (a "master copy" of
weights). You get the bandwidth and capacity wins where data is bulky, and
keep precision exactly where errors compound. Lab L3.2 makes this concrete:
you'll implement fp16 and bf16 rounding by hand from bit patterns and check
yourself against PyTorch.

## Where this goes next

Sixteen bits is not the floor. Inference pushes to 8 bits and below — but
below fp8, floating point itself stops paying rent and a different trick
takes over: integers with a scale factor. That's the next lesson.
