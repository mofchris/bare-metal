## Why shrink numbers at all?

Lesson 01 gave float32 ~7 digits of precision — far more than a neural network
needs. Its inputs are noisy to begin with, and its weights tolerate being
nudged slightly; nothing about the task demands seven digits.

Meanwhile M1 taught that most tensor operations are memory-bound. Halving the
bytes per number therefore buys three things at once: it halves memory traffic
(doubling arithmetic intensity, sliding the operation rightward on the
roofline toward the compute-bound side), it halves memory footprint (bigger
models and batches fit), and it unlocks hardware that multiplies narrow
numbers faster than wide ones. Precision is a _budget you spend_; the question
is only where to cut.

## Two ways to split 16 bits

| Format   | Exponent | Mantissa | Range                                  | Precision (~decimal) |
| -------- | -------- | -------- | -------------------------------------- | -------------------- |
| float32  | 8        | 23       | ~10^±38                                | ~7 digits            |
| **fp16** | 5        | 10       | max **65504**, smallest normal ~6×10⁻⁵ | ~3 digits            |
| **bf16** | 8        | 7        | ~10^±38 (same as fp32)                 | ~2–3 digits          |

Same 16 bits, opposite philosophies. **fp16** keeps decent precision but pays
with a tiny range: anything past 65504 **overflows** — exceeds what the format
can hold, and lands on infinity — while very small values **underflow**,
getting flushed to zero because there's no exponent low enough to express
them. **bf16** ("brain float") is float32 with the last 16 mantissa bits sawn
off: the _same_ exponent field, so the same enormous range — paying with
precision so coarse that 256 + 1 rounds back to 256.

## Why range beat precision in training

Three words this argument needs, each properly built in M5 but load-bearing
right now.

Training works by measuring how wrong the model currently is — that single
number is the **loss** — and then computing, for every weight in the model,
which direction and how hard to nudge it to make the loss smaller. Those
per-weight nudges are the **gradients**, and the return sweep that computes
them all is the **backward pass**, or **backprop**. Training is then a loop:
forward to get the loss, backward to get the gradients, adjust the weights,
repeat until the loss stops improving (at which point the model has
**converged**).

Gradients are training's problem children, because they span wild magnitudes:
they shrink toward zero as the model converges and occasionally spike. In
fp16, the small ones underflow to zero, which means those weights receive a
nudge of exactly nothing and silently stop learning; the spikes overflow to
infinity, and infinity poisons the arithmetic downstream until the loss
becomes NaN. Mixed-precision training with fp16 works — Micikevicius et al.
showed it — but only with **loss scaling** (lesson 04) constantly shepherding
values into fp16's narrow window.

bf16's fp32-sized range makes the shepherding unnecessary: nothing that fit in
fp32 overflows or underflows in bf16. The coarse mantissa turns out to be fine
because the random variation already present in gradients (they're estimated
from a batch, not the full dataset) exceeds the rounding error, and the sums
that genuinely need care are done in fp32 anyway. That practical simplicity is
why bf16, introduced for Google's TPUs (their in-house ML accelerators),
became the training default across modern hardware.

## The pattern to remember

Modern precision policy in one sentence: **store and move tensors narrow;
accumulate wide**. An **accumulator** is the running total that a long chain
of products gets summed into — and it's kept in a wider format than its
inputs, because that's where small errors would otherwise pile up. Matmul
units consume bf16/fp16 inputs but sum their products into fp32 accumulators;
the **optimizer** — the rule that turns gradients into the actual weight
update, built properly in M5 — often keeps its own bookkeeping in fp32,
holding a full-precision "master copy" of the weights that the narrow working
copy is derived from each step.

The whole arrangement is called **mixed precision**: you get the bandwidth and
capacity wins where data is bulky, and keep precision exactly where errors
compound. Lab L3.2 makes this concrete: you'll implement fp16 and bf16
rounding by hand from bit patterns and check yourself against PyTorch.

## Where this goes next

Sixteen bits is not the floor. Inference pushes to 8 bits and below — but
below that, floating point itself stops paying rent and a different trick
