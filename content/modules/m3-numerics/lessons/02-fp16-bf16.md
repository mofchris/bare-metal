## What this lesson answers

Lesson 01 showed that float32 spends 32 bits to give about 7 decimal digits of
precision. This lesson asks whether a neural network needs all of them, finds
that it does not, and then works out which half of the budget to cut.

Two different answers to that question exist in hardware. Understanding why one
of them won is the point of the lesson.

## Why would you want fewer bits per number?

A neural network tolerates imprecise numbers. Its inputs are noisy
measurements, and its weights are found by a search that arrives at approximate
values, so nothing about the task demands seven digits.

Meanwhile, M1 established that most tensor operations are memory-bound. Halving
the bytes per number therefore buys three separate things.

It halves the bytes moved, which doubles arithmetic intensity and slides the
operation rightward on the roofline toward its compute-bound side. It halves
memory footprint, so larger models and larger batches fit. And it lets hardware
built for narrow numbers multiply them faster than wide ones.

So precision is a budget you spend rather than a quantity you maximise. The
question is only which part to cut.

## What are the two ways to split 16 bits?

| Format   | Exponent | Mantissa | Range                                  | Precision (~decimal) |
| -------- | -------- | -------- | -------------------------------------- | -------------------- |
| float32  | 8        | 23       | ~10^±38                                | ~7 digits            |
| **fp16** | 5        | 10       | max **65504**, smallest normal ~6×10⁻⁵ | ~3 digits            |
| **bf16** | 8        | 7        | ~10^±38 (same as fp32)                 | ~2–3 digits          |

The two formats take opposite positions with the same 16 bits.

**fp16** keeps a reasonable mantissa and pays with range. Its 5-bit exponent
reaches only to 65504, so a value beyond that **overflows**, meaning it exceeds
what the format can express and becomes infinity. At the other end, values
below about 6 × 10⁻⁵ **underflow**, meaning no exponent is small enough to
express them and they collapse to zero.

**bf16** is float32 with 16 mantissa bits removed. It keeps the full 8-bit
exponent, so its range is identical to float32's, and it pays with precision so
coarse that 256 + 1 rounds back to 256.

## What does training actually do?

The argument for one format over the other depends on three ideas that M5
builds properly. They are needed here, so here they are.

Training measures how wrong a model currently is, and that measurement is a
single number called the **loss**. It then computes, for every weight, which
direction to nudge that weight and how hard, in order to make the loss smaller.
Those per-weight nudges are the **gradients**.

The sweep that computes all the gradients is the **backward pass**, also called
**backprop**. Training is then a loop: run the model forward to get the loss,
run backward to get the gradients, adjust the weights, repeat. When the loss
stops improving, the model has **converged**.

## Why did range beat precision?

Gradients are the problem, and their behaviour is what decides the format.

Gradient magnitudes span an enormous range. Early in training they are large.
As a model converges they shrink toward zero, because the weights are nearly
where they should be. Occasionally one spikes.

Now run those magnitudes through fp16's narrow window. A small gradient
underflows to zero, which means that weight receives a nudge of exactly
nothing and stops learning, and no error is raised. A spike overflows to
infinity, and infinity contaminates everything computed from it until the loss
becomes NaN.

Training in fp16 is possible, and Micikevicius and colleagues showed how, but
it requires **loss scaling** to hold values inside the window at every step.
Lesson 04 covers that machinery.

bf16 removes the need for it entirely. Its range equals float32's, so anything
that fit in float32 fits in bf16 without overflow or underflow, and no
shepherding is required.

The obvious objection is that bf16's mantissa is tiny. In practice that does
not hurt, for two reasons. A gradient is estimated from one batch rather than
the whole dataset, so it already carries random variation larger than bf16's
rounding error. And the sums that genuinely need accuracy are not performed in
bf16 at all, which is the next section.

That combination is why bf16, introduced by Google for its TPUs, became the
default for training across modern hardware.

## What is the rule that makes narrow formats safe?

The policy is: store and move numbers narrow, add them up wide.

An **accumulator** is the running total that a long chain of products is summed
into. Matrix multiplication reads bf16 or fp16 inputs, but it accumulates the
products into a float32 total, because that is where rounding errors would
otherwise pile up across hundreds of additions.

The same principle applies to the weights. A full-precision float32 copy is
kept, called the **master copy**, and the narrow working copy used for
computation is derived from it each step. Updates are applied to the master
copy, so a nudge too small to register in bf16 is not lost.

The whole arrangement is called **mixed precision**. You take the bandwidth and
capacity savings wherever data is bulky, and you keep full precision at the
specific points where errors accumulate.

## Check your understanding

During training in fp16, an engineer sees the loss stop improving while nothing
appears to crash. Later in the same run, the loss becomes NaN.

Explain both symptoms in terms of fp16's 5-bit exponent, and say which single
change to the number format would remove both without needing loss scaling. A
correct answer attributes the plateau to small gradients underflowing fp16's
floor of about 6 × 10⁻⁵ and becoming zero, so those weights stop being updated
silently; attributes the NaN to a gradient spike exceeding 65504 and becoming
infinity, which then contaminates later arithmetic; and identifies switching to
bf16, whose 8-bit exponent gives float32's range, as the change that removes
both.
